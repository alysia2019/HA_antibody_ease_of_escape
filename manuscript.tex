\section*{Abstract}
There is great interest in broad antibody-based immunity against influenza virus.
Even for antibodies recognizing broad swaths of influenza strains, viral genetic variation can influence neutralization sensitivity.
To better understand the potential for viral resistance to antibodies, there is need to evaluate not just whether mutations can influence sensitivity to an antibody, but also to quantify how many mutations do this and by how much, and to make these comparisons across antibodies.
Here we comprehensively map mutations that increase viral resistance to the broadly neutralizing anti-influenza antibody FI6v3, and compare the results to those previously obtained for narrow strain-specific antibodies.
We find that although there are some mutations that enhance resistance to FI6v3, these mutations are both rare and modest in their effect sizes.
We formalize these observations into a framework for quantitative comparison of viral escape potential between different antibodies.
Overall, our work demonstrates that a virus's absolute capacity to escape an antibody can be quantified, and that influenza is in fact extremely limited in its ability to escape some antibodies.
